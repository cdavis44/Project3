Bz: Strength of the magnetic field in the North-South direction. If positive, the IMF is pointing in the same direction as earth's magnetic field. Results in minimal or no auroral activity. This can be related to two magnets lined up repelling each other. When Bz is negative, the IMF points southward which is the opposite direction of earth's magnetic field. This will result in vibrant auroras.


Bt: Strength of the Interplanetary Magnetic Field (IMF). The stronger the IMF is, the stronger the auroras. There is one condition to this: Bz must be negative for auroras to be present. Since Bz implies direction, the IMF value is only relevant for causing auroras when Bz points in the proper, southward direction. IMF simply relates to magnitude when related to a vector.

Kp: Index that describes disturbance in earth's magnetic field caused by solar wind. The faster the solar wind blows, the greater the turbulence. Ranges from 0-9, 0 being lowest "quietest" value and 9 being the largest "most intense" value for geomagnetic storm.
